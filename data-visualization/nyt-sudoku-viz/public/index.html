Let's start with my strategy

When I first see a sudoku grid, my instinct is to look for naked singles. Naked singles are cells where there can be only one possible value. They're the slam dunks. No-brainers. Easy pickings. (They‚Äôre also called sole candidates, but why would you call them that when you could say naked singles? I digress.)

In the NYTimes grid above, only the number 9 can be played in the highlighted cell.

Using the process of elimination, I identify naked singles and solve those cells. I only ever fill in a value when it‚Äôs a naked single, i.e., I never guess. So when I fill out the grid, I almost never redo cells once I fill them. This is a tradition I‚Äôve digitized ‚Äî high school me would fill out grids with a pen.

As you solve each cell in the sudoku, you eliminate possible values throughout the grid. For example, putting a 5 in one cell means you can‚Äôt play a 5 anywhere else in that row, column, or section. In the process of filling out cells, you create more naked singles.

Essentially, by getting the numbers of these naked singles, you're introduced to their other naked single friends.

And this is a great way to analyze my performance.

Based on the analysis above, I quickly solve cells that are towards the top, then take longer to fill out cells that are in the middle and bottom.

Why do I tend to take longer in these areas? I think it has something to do with how the cells come prefilled. Let's talk about that.

Patterns

Like Jeopardy! and its daily doubles, the Times‚Äô sudoku grids tend to have the same cells prefilled. For the 500 sudoku grids I have prefilled data for, the easy sudoku has always come with exactly 38 values prefilled.

Data above is from 500 Times sudokus. Hover over each cell above to see what percent of grids it came prefilled for.

From the grid above, we see that the typical NYT easy sudoku has more prefilled cells towards the top. This makes it easier to start from the top. In fact, of the 100 sudokus I've collected data for, my first move is in the top three rows in 73 of them.

The higher density of cells also makes it easier for my next strategy: slicing and dicing. Slicing and dicing is a common maneuver where, once you find a blank cell, you quickly scan its row and its column to see what numbers that cell can't be. In fact, in the primer on naked singles above, you could solve the empty cell by slicing and dicing, rather than cycling through all nine numbers.

When slicing and dicing, you look at a cell's column and row to determine what numbers that cell can't be.

Taken together, slicing and dicing and looking for naked singles are my primary strategies when solving the sudoku. I also look for something called hidden singles, but those are much harder to analyze. If you‚Äôre curious about them, read up here.

Do I stick to some numbers more than others?

Even though there are 10 basic digits that all numbers are made of, they‚Äôre not always uniform. Benford‚Äôs Law, for example, says that the leading digit in numbers of a dataset is typically small.

I tend to start with the number ‚Äú1‚Äù most of the time. While it isn't close to Benford's Law, it's still skewed towards smaller numbers. In fact, in the 84 sudokus I have data for3, I started with the number ‚Äú1‚Äù for 32 of them.

Mistakes happen. Everybody makes them. But you don't want to repeat your mistakes and not learn from them. Is there a pattern with when I make mistakes? Let‚Äôs highlight all my mistakes, i.e., cells that I filled out more than once.

I‚Äôm more likely to make mistakes when I‚Äôm almost done solving a puzzle. This is most likely because I get impatient. Sometimes I also guess because it‚Äôs faster than slicing and dicing.

There are also two kinds of mistakes:

  2. ones where I put in the wrong value and actually think it's right

I made a mistake in 65% of the puzzles I solved. It‚Äôs not only important to make fewer mistakes, but also to reduce the amount of time it takes to correct them. When I make a mistake, it costs me a median of 5.1 seconds.

Other observations

When slicing and dicing, I first read across the cell's row, and then its column. I've noticed that I always take longer to scan its column because it's harder to track numbers when reading from top to bottom, as opposed to left to right. To overcome this, I'll probably have to develop quicker mental models of scanning.

And because I rely so heavily on scanning a cell's row and column, I sometimes forget to scan its section. Remembering to do this could save me a few seconds, especially when I move towards the middle and bottom of the grid.

Quicker scanning leads to fewer mistakes, and that leads to a faster finish time!
